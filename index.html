01 · Abstract

Overview

Recent advances in deep learning, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have significantly enhanced medical image classification. However, model performance is influenced not only by network architecture but also by the choice of optimizer. This study systematically evaluates and compares four optimizers — Adam, AdamW, NAdam, and RMSprop — across six CNN architectures: EfficientNet-B2, EfficientNet-B4, EfficientNetV2-S, EfficientNetV2-M, ResNet50V2, and Xception for microscopic blood cell image classification in ALL detection.

The experiments were conducted using the publicly available ALL dataset consisting of 3,242 colored blood cell images from 89 patients across four classes. Results indicate that Xception combined with RMSprop achieved the highest performance, reaching 99.69% accuracy with a loss of 0.0194, while ResNet50V2 showed the lowest accuracy (93.85%). The primary contribution lies in quantifying the interaction effects between optimizer and architecture choices, providing empirical guidance for developing more effective CNN-based diagnostic systems for ALL detection.

Keywords: Deep Learning · CNN · Optimizer · Xception · RMSprop

Why Xception?

Xception leverages depthwise separable convolutions, which efficiently decouple channel-wise and spatial feature extraction. This architectural design offers excellent representational power ideal for capturing fine morphological differences in blood cell imagery.

Why RMSprop?

RMSprop adapts learning rates using a moving average of squared gradients, stabilizing training on small, imbalanced medical datasets. It avoids the aggressive weight decay of AdamW and the potential over-correction of NAdam, making it ideal for this task.

05 · Conclusion

Key Findings

🎯

Best Combination

Xception + RMSprop achieved the highest accuracy of 99.69% with a test loss of 0.0194 — the strongest result among all 24 architecture–optimizer pairs.

📉

Lowest Performer

ResNet50V2 consistently performed lowest across all optimizers, with a best accuracy of 96.00% using Adam. This suggests its residual architecture is less suited to fine-grained blood cell morphology.

🔄

Optimizer Impact

The choice of optimizer significantly interacts with architecture. RMSprop excels with Xception, while NAdam shows competitive performance with EfficientNetV2 variants. No single optimizer dominates universally.

🏥

Clinical Relevance

Results empirically demonstrate that Xception + RMSprop can serve as a reliable backbone for automated ALL diagnosis systems, supporting computer-assisted clinical decision-making in hematology.

⚠️

Limitations

Experiments use a single dataset; splitting is image-level, not patient-level. Only 6 architectures and 4 optimizers were evaluated. Future work should extend to more diverse datasets and patient-aware validation splits.

🔬

Future Directions

Exploring patient-level cross-validation, additional architectures (e.g., ViT, DenseNet), and ensemble methods may further improve reliability and generalization for clinical deployment.
